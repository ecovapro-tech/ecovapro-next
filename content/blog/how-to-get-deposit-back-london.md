The average UK tenancy deposit deduction in 2025 was £215. In London, where deposits are larger and standards are higher, the median deduction is closer to £350. Most of these deductions are avoidable. This guide walks through how London's deposit protection system actually works, what landlords can legally deduct, and what to do if you disagree.

## The basics: where your deposit goes

If you're on an assured shorthold tenancy that started after April 2007, your deposit must be held in one of three government-backed protection schemes:

- **DPS** (Deposit Protection Service)
- **MyDeposits**
- **TDS** (Tenancy Deposit Scheme)

Your landlord or agent has 30 days from receipt to protect the deposit and give you the prescribed information. If they didn't, you have a strong claim — they can be forced to repay between 1× and 3× the deposit value to you, plus the original sum.

## What landlords can deduct (and what they can't)

Three legitimate categories:

1. **Damage beyond fair wear and tear** — a hole in a wall, a stained carpet, a broken cupboard door. Marks on walls from picture hooks are usually wear and tear.
2. **Unpaid rent or bills** — straightforward, hard to dispute if true.
3. **Cleaning costs to return the property to its check-in condition** — this is the most disputed category and the one we see clients lose deposits over most often.

Three things landlords frequently try to deduct that are not legitimate:

- **General redecoration after a long tenancy.** Carpets after 7 years, paint after 5 — these are fair wear and tear, not damage.
- **Administrative fees** beyond reasonable cost.
- **"Professional cleaning" as a requirement** — the Tenant Fees Act 2019 made it illegal to require a professional clean in your tenancy agreement (unless one was provided at check-in, in which case parity is required).

## The check-in report is your most important document

The inventory check-in report — the photos and descriptions taken on the day you moved in — is the document everything is compared against. Two practical implications:

1. **Read it carefully on day one.** If anything is misrepresented or missing, dispute it within seven days. Once you've accepted the inventory, it's the source of truth.
2. **At check-out, photograph everything that was photographed at check-in.** Same angle, same lighting where possible. Date-stamped photos are powerful evidence if a deduction is disputed.

## The five most common deduction reasons (and how to prevent them)

1. **The oven.** Inventory clerks check every oven, every time. If yours isn't clean to professional standard, this alone causes a £40–£80 deduction. A professional clean takes 90 minutes; a re-do costs much more than doing it once.
2. **Limescale in the bathroom.** Showerheads, taps, glass screens. London water is very hard. Descale before check-out using a citric-acid based product (vinegar works but smells).
3. **Carpets.** If your check-in listed "professionally steam-cleaned carpets", you need to return them in that condition. Hoovering is not enough.
4. **Garden / outside space.** Often missed entirely. If you have a garden, it needs to be returned to check-in condition — mowed, weeded, bins emptied.
5. **Walls.** Picture-hook holes are usually fine. Fresh paint marks, scuffs at child height, deep dents are not. Use a magic eraser sponge for scuffs.

## Disputing a deduction: what actually wins

If you receive a deduction notice you don't agree with, you have the right to dispute via your deposit scheme's free adjudication service. Statistics from DPS suggest tenants win roughly 40% of dispute decisions in part or in full. What separates winning from losing cases:

- **Evidence over argument.** Photos with timestamps beat words. A picture of a clean oven at check-out beats five paragraphs explaining you cleaned it.
- **Specific objections, not general ones.** "I dispute the £200 cleaning charge because the check-in report shows the property was not professionally cleaned at the start of the tenancy, and the Tenant Fees Act 2019 prevents requiring this at check-out" is much stronger than "I cleaned it before I left".
- **Engagement on quotes.** Many deductions are based on a single supplier quote. You can challenge the quote with independent quotes of your own.

The deposit scheme will not release any disputed funds until the adjudication is complete. The undisputed portion is released within 10 days of the parties agreeing on it.

## Practical pre-checkout timeline

**Two weeks before:**
- Re-read your check-in inventory report
- Book your professional clean (most demand exceeds supply at month-end — book early)
- List anything you'll need to repair or replace

**One week before:**
- Repair small damage you can fix yourself (filler for picture hooks, magic eraser for scuffs)
- Start packing — empty cupboards and behind furniture get cleaned properly when accessible

**Day before check-out:**
- Property is empty, all rubbish removed
- Take meter readings, photograph them
- Cleaners arrive, work to inventory standard
- Photograph every room post-clean

**Day of check-out:**
- Hand over keys
- Email the agent your meter readings, photos, and a brief note that the property is left in the condition documented in your photos

## A practical note on professional cleaning

If your tenancy started with a professionally cleaned property (the inventory will say so explicitly), professional cleaning at check-out gives you the strongest possible evidence in any cleaning-related dispute. Look for:

- A company that issues an invoice (not just a Venmo confirmation)
- A 48-hour re-clean guarantee
- A receipt itemising what was cleaned

Getting your deposit back in London isn't luck. It's three things: understanding the rules, photographing everything, and being properly prepared for check-out. Most deductions are preventable. Most disputes that go to adjudication, the well-prepared party wins.
