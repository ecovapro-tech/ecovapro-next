Most people assume eco cleaning products are a tradeoff: you save the planet, you accept a worse result. As a London cleaning company that uses plant-based products on hundreds of jobs a year — including the most demanding end of tenancy cleans — that hasn't been our experience. But we also won't pretend everything labelled "eco" is equal. Here's what actually works.

## Where eco products match or beat conventional ones

**Day-to-day surface cleaning.** Plant-based all-purpose sprays based on coconut-derived surfactants clean kitchen surfaces, hobs, bathroom tiles, and woodwork as well as anything else on the market. The difference in lab-tested cleaning performance for routine grime is essentially zero.

**Glass and mirrors.** Vinegar plus a tiny amount of plant-based detergent outperforms most branded glass sprays for streak-free finishes.

**Wood and laminate floors.** Plant-based concentrates diluted in warm water leave hard floors looking better than ammonia-based products — and they don't strip the finish over time.

**Most fabric cleaning.** Enzyme-based plant detergents handle organic stains (food, blood, sweat) better than basic chemical alternatives because they're literally designed to break down the proteins involved.

## Where you need to pick the right eco product

**Limescale.** Vinegar works on light limescale but takes contact time and effort. For heavy bathroom limescale — the kind that builds up in London hard-water bathrooms after months — citric-acid-based concentrates are what we use. They're plant-derived, biodegradable, and dissolve heavy buildup in 5–10 minutes. Look for concentrates labelled "citric acid" specifically.

**Oven grease.** This is where cheap eco products fail and conventional ones still feel necessary. The trick is a plant-based oven cleaner using sodium percarbonate (essentially a more concentrated, oxygen-based bleach derived from soda crystals) — it cuts baked-on grease as effectively as caustic conventional cleaners, without the fume mask. We use it on every end of tenancy oven. Cheap "eco" oven sprays that just contain dish soap and citric acid don't work on heavy grease.

**Mould.** Black mould genuinely is hard to remove without bleach or hydrogen peroxide. Hydrogen peroxide is plant-compatible, biodegradable, and effective — and unlike bleach, it doesn't off-gas chlorine fumes. Use 3% (the supermarket strength). It needs 10–15 minutes contact time.

**Drains and waste pipes.** Caustic drain cleaners are nasty for the environment. Bicarbonate of soda followed by boiling water and vinegar works for routine maintenance. For genuinely blocked pipes, you need a manual unblocking — no eco product fixes a serious blockage and neither does a chemical one without environmental cost.

## What "eco" actually means (and what to ignore)

The cleaning products industry is full of greenwashing. Three signals are meaningful:

1. **Certifications by independent bodies.** EU Ecolabel, Cradle to Cradle, NaTrue. These actually verify ingredient lists. "Eco-friendly" with no certification on the bottle means nothing.
2. **Biodegradability in days, not months.** Look for OECD 301 test references.
3. **No synthetic fragrance.** Synthetic fragrance is the largest source of indoor air pollution from cleaning products. Plant-derived essential oils for scent are fine, no scent is better.

Ignore "natural" — water is natural, arsenic is natural. Ignore "non-toxic" — bleach is non-toxic at the right dilution. Ignore green packaging — that's marketing.

## What this means for you

If you're booking a cleaning service and you care about indoor air quality, plant-based products, or having a clean that's safe around children, pets, or allergies, the right question to ask isn't "do you use eco products?" — most companies will say yes. The right question is "which specific products do you use, and what do you use for limescale, oven grease, and mould?"

A company with a real answer is a company that's thought about it. A company that mumbles general assurances is a company that uses whatever's cheapest at Costco.
